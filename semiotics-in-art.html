Semiotics

Reading Images

Semiotics is the study of signs — how meaning is made, not just what things look like. Applied to art, it asks: what are images actually saying, and how do they say it?

Foundation

The Sign: Saussure's Big Idea

Ferdinand de Saussure (1857–1913) proposed that all communication works through signs. A sign is not a thing — it's a relationship between two parts. Understanding this is the key that unlocks semiotic analysis.

Roland Barthes later extended this to images: a photograph, a painting, an advertisement — all are systems of signs that can be "read" like a language.

1
The whole unit
The Sign
The complete unit of meaning — the pairing of a sound/image with a concept. A sign is always relational, never isolated.
The word "pipe" as a whole unit
2
The form
Signifier
The physical form the sign takes — the sounds, marks, or visual elements that carry the meaning. The "what you see or hear."
The painted image of a pipe
3
The concept
Signified
The mental concept or idea that the signifier evokes. Not the actual object — the idea of it that exists in our minds.
Our concept of "pipe-ness"

Crucially, Saussure argued the link between signifier and signified is arbitrary — there's nothing pipe-like about the letters P-I-P-E. The connection is a social convention, agreed upon by a culture. This means meanings can shift, be contested, or differ between cultures entirely.

Case Study

Magritte's Trap: The Treachery of Images

La Trahison des images (The Treachery of Images)
René Magritte · 1929 · Oil on canvas · Los Angeles County Museum of Art

This is arguably the most famous semiotic joke in art history. Magritte painted a meticulously realistic pipe, then wrote beneath it — in neat schoolroom cursive — "Ceci n'est pas une pipe": "This is not a pipe."

Is he wrong? Click through the layers below to see what semiotics reveals.

Layer 01 —
What you see

A painted pipe

The image depicts a tobacco pipe with startling realism. Your brain immediately recognizes it. ↓ click to explore

The signifier here is the arrangement of paint on canvas — curved shapes, brown tones, a certain silhouette — that trigger your mental concept of "pipe." The painting is so convincing that your first instinct is to accept it as representing reality. This is exactly the habit Magritte wants to disrupt.
Layer 02 —
The text

"This is not a pipe"

The sentence contradicts what you see — or does it? ↓ click to explore

Magritte is technically correct. You cannot stuff tobacco into this pipe. You cannot hold it. What you see is paint — a representation, a sign that refers to a pipe. The painting uses the pipe's signifier (visual form) without delivering the signified (the actual object). Writing confronts you with this gap. The text is a second sign system placed in tension with the image sign system.
Layer 03 —
The trap

The image of language

But here's the twist Magritte hid in plain sight. ↓ click to explore

The sentence "Ceci n'est pas une pipe" is also not a pipe. It's marks on canvas — a painted representation of handwritten words. The word "pipe" in the sentence is a signifier, too, not the thing itself. Magritte has stacked sign upon sign, drawing our attention to the infinite regress of representation. Nothing in the painting is what it appears to be — including the denial.
Layer 04 —
The argument

What Magritte is claiming

The painting is a philosophical statement about representation itself. ↓ click to explore

Magritte is arguing that images always betray their subjects — they are never the thing, only a sign of the thing. We habitually confuse the sign for the reality. This is the "treachery" of images: they appear transparent (showing us the world) while actually being opaque (constructions of meaning). Semiotics makes this betrayal visible. Every image is a translation, not a window.
Barthes

Denotation & Connotation

Roland Barthes extended Saussure's framework to popular culture and art. He introduced a two-level model of meaning. Take any image — say, an advertisement showing a woman in a red dress against a stark white background.

Level 1

Denotation

What the image literally shows — objective, descriptive, apparently neutral.

  • A woman standing
  • She wears a red dress
  • The background is white
  • She is looking at the camera
  • Her posture is upright
Level 2

Connotation

What the image culturally implies — loaded with social, historical, emotional meaning.

  • Red = passion, danger, desire
  • White background = purity, luxury, focus
  • Direct gaze = confidence, invitation
  • Upright posture = power, status
  • The combination = aspirational femininity

Barthes' crucial point: connotation disguises itself as denotation. We experience culturally loaded meanings as if they were simply "what's there." Semiotics pulls back that curtain — making the cultural work of images visible.

Applied to fine art: when Vermeer paints a woman reading a letter by a window, the denotation is simply that scene. The connotations — solitude, feminine interiority, the outside world glimpsed but inaccessible, the intimacy of private correspondence — are a cultural language that 17th-century Dutch viewers would have read fluently.

Barthes — Mythologies (1957)

When Connotation Becomes Myth

Barthes introduced a third concept: myth. When a connotation becomes so naturalized that it seems inevitable — when culture passes itself off as nature — a myth is formed. Myth is ideology made invisible.

Signifier (form)
Image of a soldier saluting a French flag
→
Signified (concept)
"Soldier," "flag," "military"
First-order sign: straightforward recognition
Myth (second order) — the whole first sign becomes a new signifier
"France is a great multi-ethnic empire whose sons serve her faithfully regardless of background"
Barthes' actual example from Mythologies. The image naturalizes French imperialism — presenting colonial loyalty as organic and inevitable, rather than as a political construction.

In fine art, myth operates constantly. Landscapes painted as timeless and serene mythologize land ownership as natural. Heroic portraits mythologize power as deserved. Genre paintings of "happy peasants" mythologize class hierarchy as contentment.

The semiotic task is to demythologize — to ask: what naturalized political claim is this image making? What does it present as inevitable that is actually constructed?
