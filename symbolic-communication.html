Symbolic
Communication

A field guide to signs, meanings & the world between them

∑

Symbolic communication is the use of signs — words, gestures, images, objects, sounds — to convey meaning beyond their literal form. It is the foundation of all human language, culture, and thought.

01 — What
The Core Idea

A symbol is anything that stands for something else through convention or resemblance. The word "tree" is not a tree. A red light is not danger itself. Symbols create shared mental bridges between minds.

02 — Why
Why It Matters

Without symbols, humans could only communicate about what is immediately present. Symbols allow abstraction: past, future, hypotheticals, emotions, gods, numbers — none of which exist in the physical world.

03 — How
The Mechanism

Meaning emerges from shared agreement. A symbol only works if sender and receiver both recognize its significance. This shared code — learned through culture — is what allows communication to happen at all.

04 — Where
Everywhere

Language, mathematics, music, art, ritual, law, flags, money, brand logos, body language, emojis — all are systems of symbolic communication. Humans are, fundamentally, symbol-making animals.

The Triangle of Meaning

The semiotic triangle shows how meaning is constructed. There is no direct connection between a symbol (the word or sign) and the referent (the real thing) — the link is always mediated by thought (the mental concept).

← Click a node to explore each corner

Thought / Concept

The mental image or idea that mediates between sign and world. When you see the word "apple," your mind constructs a concept — colour, taste, shape, associations — before linking it to any actual apple.

This is why meaning can differ between people: the same symbol activates different conceptual associations depending on experience, culture, and context.

e.g. your mental model of "home" the idea of "justice"

Symbol / Sign

The physical form — spoken word, written mark, gesture, image — that triggers meaning. The symbol itself has no inherent meaning; it only signifies through convention and agreement.

The word "dog" and the word "chien" (French) both refer to the same concept, proving the link is arbitrary.

spoken words written text gestures icons

Referent / Object

The actual thing or phenomenon in the world (or imagination) that the symbol ultimately points to. The referent is independent of the symbol — the real dog doesn't know it's called "dog."

Crucially, referents can be entirely abstract: "freedom," "the number seven," "yesterday" — things with no physical form at all.

physical objects events abstract concepts

Types of Symbols

Peirce's triadic classification — hover to explore

🖼
Peirce Type I
Icon

Resembles what it represents. The connection is one of similarity — you can see the link without being taught it.

e.g. a portrait, a map, onomatopoeia, a smiley face emoji

👆
Peirce Type II
Index

Has a direct, causal or physical link to its referent. Points to something by being connected to it in the world.

e.g. smoke → fire; a fever → illness; pointing; a footprint

🔤
Peirce Type III
Symbol

The connection is purely conventional — arbitrary, learned, and agreed upon by a community. Most language works this way.

e.g. words, flags, traffic lights, currency signs

🤝
By Convention
Conventional Sign

Broader term for any sign whose meaning depends on social agreement — overlaps with Peirce's "symbol" but emphasizes the communal contract.

e.g. handshake, wedding ring, the color red in many cultures

✋
Embodied
Gesture & Kinesics

Bodily movements as symbols. Some are universal (pain), many are culturally specific (thumbs up, A-OK). Body language is a rich symbolic system.

e.g. nodding, bowing, eyebrow flash, proxemics

🎵
Acoustic
Sound Symbol

Sounds as carriers of meaning beyond their acoustics. Can be iconic (birdsong in music = nature) or purely conventional (national anthems).

e.g. alarms, fanfares, musical leitmotifs, signal sounds

Domains of Symbolic Communication

Where symbol systems operate across human life

🗣 Language
The most complex symbolic system humans possess. Words, grammar, and syntax form layered codes that allow the expression of virtually any concept. Language is arbitrary, productive, and culturally situated.
spoken written signed
🔢 Mathematics
A formal symbolic language of precise, universal meaning. Mathematical symbols (∑, ∞, π) transcend spoken language barriers but require shared training to decode. Perhaps the most unambiguous symbol system ever devised.
universal formal
🎨 Visual Art
Art operates through iconic resemblance, cultural symbol (the halo, the skull), and pure aesthetic form. Meaning is often polysemous — deliberately open to multiple interpretations — unlike linguistic signs.
iconic cultural open
🎶 Music
Music uses sound indexically (minor keys → sadness through cultural convention), iconically (tremolo → fear), and symbolically (national anthems). It is the most emotionally direct of all symbolic systems.
emotional cultural
⛪ Ritual & Religion
Dense symbolic environments where objects, gestures, words, and space all carry sacred or community-binding meaning. Symbols here mediate between the human and the transcendent — or between individual and collective identity.
sacred communal
📱 Digital Media
A rapidly evolving symbolic ecology: emojis, memes, interface icons, hashtags, and algorithmic signals. Digital symbols often blend iconic, indexical, and symbolic dimensions and evolve faster than any previous symbol system in history.
emoji meme UX icons

Key Thinkers

The minds who shaped our understanding of symbols and meaning

S
Ferdinand de Saussure
1857–1913 · Structural Linguistics

Proposed that the linguistic sign has two inseparable sides: the signifier (sound-image) and the signified (concept). Crucially, the link between them is arbitrary — there's no natural reason "tree" means tree. This arbitrariness is what makes language a social institution.

"In language there are only differences, without positive terms."
P
Charles Sanders Peirce
1839–1914 · Pragmatist Semiotics

Developed the triadic model of sign: representamen, object, interpretant. Classified signs as icon, index, or symbol. Argued that all thought is sign-based — we cannot think outside of signs. Founded semiotics as a formal discipline.

"The universe is perfused with signs, if it is not composed exclusively of signs."
C
Ernst Cassirer
1874–1945 · Philosophy of Symbolic Forms

Argued that humans are not rational animals but symbolic animals (animal symbolicum). Language, myth, art, science — all are symbolic forms through which humans construct and inhabit their world. No symbol-free access to reality is possible for us.

"Man lives in a symbolic universe."
R
Roland Barthes
1915–1980 · Cultural Semiology

Extended semiotic analysis to popular culture — wrestling, advertising, food, fashion. Showed that ideology works through symbols by naturalizing cultural constructions. Introduced the concept of myth as second-order signification: signs become signifiers for ideological concepts.

"Myth does not deny things; on the contrary, its function is to talk about them."
V
Lev Vygotsky
1896–1934 · Developmental Psychology

Showed that symbolic tools — primarily language — are not just outputs of thought but constitute and shape thought itself. The inner "verbal thought" that underlies much of human cognition is symbolic communication turned inward.

"Thought is not merely expressed in words; it comes into existence through them."
U
Umberto Eco
1932–2016 · Semiotics & Literature

Developed a comprehensive general semiotics bridging Saussure and Peirce. Famous for the concept of "unlimited semiosis" — signs always refer to other signs in an infinite chain. Also explored how texts generate meaning through reader interpretation.

"A book is a machine to think with."
